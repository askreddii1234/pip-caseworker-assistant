## Particulate matter — PM2.5

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) refers to airborne particles with an
aerodynamic diameter of 2.5 micrometres or less. PM2.5 penetrates the deep
lung and the bloodstream and is associated with increased all-cause
mortality, cardiovascular disease, and reduced lung function development in
children.

WHO recommended levels:

- **Annual mean: 5 µg/m³**
- **24-hour mean: 15 µg/m³** (99th percentile, i.e. 3–4 exceedance days/year)

Interim targets recognise that many regions cannot achieve the recommended
levels immediately. Interim Target 4 corresponds to 10 µg/m³ annual / 25
µg/m³ daily and is the closest tier to the UK's current ambient air quality
objectives.

## Particulate matter — PM10

PM10 (particles ≤10 µm) deposits in the upper airways. Long-term exposure is
associated with respiratory hospitalisation and aggravation of asthma.

- **Annual mean: 15 µg/m³**
- **24-hour mean: 45 µg/m³**

In school settings, PM10 sources include outdoor traffic infiltration, dust
disturbed during cleaning, and craft/technology activities.

## Nitrogen dioxide (NO2)

NO2 is primarily a traffic-related pollutant; in indoor environments it can
also be elevated near unflued gas appliances and gas hobs in catering areas.

- **Annual mean: 10 µg/m³**
- **24-hour mean: 25 µg/m³**

The UK National Air Quality Strategy (NAQS) sets an annual mean limit of 40
µg/m³, which is substantially less stringent than the WHO recommendation.
Schools located within 50m of a major road typically experience elevated NO2
on the road-facing facade and should consider mechanical ventilation with
filtration on that facade.

## Ozone (O3)

- **Peak season 8-hour mean: 60 µg/m³**
- **Short-term 8-hour mean: 100 µg/m³**

Ozone is rare indoors except where ozone-generating equipment (some
photocopiers, ionising air purifiers) is in use.

## Sulphur dioxide (SO2) and carbon monoxide (CO)

- SO2 24-hour: 40 µg/m³
- CO 24-hour: 4 mg/m³ (3500 ppb)

These are uncommon in school environments but should be considered where
combustion appliances are present without flue isolation.

## Children's vulnerability

Children inhale a higher volume of air per kilogram of body weight than
adults and their lungs continue developing through adolescence. Evidence
links chronic PM2.5 exposure to lower forced expiratory volume in the late
teens. Schools should treat sustained exceedance of WHO 24-hour means as a
Category 1 health concern, particularly for pupils with diagnosed asthma.

## Vulnerable populations

Pupils with asthma, cystic fibrosis, congenital heart conditions, or
immunocompromise are at heightened risk from particulate exposure. Where a
classroom serves a known vulnerable cohort, exceedance thresholds for action
should be applied at the WHO recommended level rather than the UK NAQS
limit.

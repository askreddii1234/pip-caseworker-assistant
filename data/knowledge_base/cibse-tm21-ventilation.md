## Purpose of CO2 monitoring

CO2 concentration in occupied spaces is the most practical proxy for
ventilation rate per occupant. A monitor logging at 1-minute intervals and
displayed in the room provides immediate feedback to teaching staff and a
record for facilities review.

The location of the monitor matters. For accurate occupant-zone readings:

- Mount the sensor between **1.1m and 1.7m** above floor level
- Avoid placement directly above radiators, in window reveals, or within
  1m of an opening window or door
- Avoid placement on external walls
- For naturally ventilated rooms, locate the sensor on the wall opposite
  the openings to capture worst-case mixing

## Trigger thresholds

CIBSE guidance, harmonised with BB101, recommends the following alert tiers
for school CO2 monitors:

- **Green: <800 ppm** — well-ventilated
- **Amber: 800–1500 ppm** — increase ventilation; open windows or boost
  mechanical supply
- **Red: >1500 ppm** — immediate action; if sustained for more than 20
  minutes, consider relocating the lesson

Where a monitor displays a 5-minute moving average rather than instantaneous
readings, the amber and red bands shift slightly upward (typically +50 ppm)
because peak transients are smoothed.

## Ventilation strategies

**Natural ventilation** — relies on opening windows and doors. Effective in
shoulder seasons but constrained in winter (heat loss) and summer
(insufficient driving force, external noise, security). Cross-ventilation is
considerably more effective than single-sided ventilation.

**Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR)** — preferred for new
build and major refurbishment. Delivers consistent outdoor air supply year
round and recovers ~80% of exhaust heat. Filters can be specified to F7 or
higher to reduce particulate ingress in roadside locations.

**Hybrid (mixed-mode)** — natural ventilation supplemented by mechanical
boost when CO2 exceeds an amber threshold. Most flexible for retrofit.

## Trend interpretation

A CO2 trace that rises rapidly during occupied periods and only drops at
break/lunch indicates ventilation rate is below the occupant load. A trace
that plateaus below 1000 ppm during occupied periods indicates adequate
ventilation. A trace that rises and remains elevated overnight or at
weekends suggests sensor malfunction or contamination from an adjacent
unoccupied space.

A 3-month rolling trend that increases by more than **5%** between
comparable occupied periods may indicate gradual ventilation system
degradation — filter loading, damper failure, or seasonal closure of trickle
vents.

## Reading sensor data alongside symptoms

Persistent occupant-reported symptoms (drowsiness, headache, eye
irritation, difficulty concentrating) in a room with sustained CO2 below
1000 ppm point to a non-CO2 cause: VOCs (new furniture, cleaning products,
markers), particulates (dust, outdoor infiltration), or thermal discomfort.

Conversely, a room with CO2 routinely above 1500 ppm but no reported
symptoms still warrants action — sub-clinical effects on concentration and
attainment are well documented in school-age cohorts.
